The Normal Section and State Training Teachers' Association

The Normal Section and State Training Teachers' Association met
jointly in room 116 of the High School Wednesday, Thursday and
Friday, December 27, 29, 1905.

On Wednesday Dr. Francis J. Cheney, Cortland Normal School, read
a paper "The Development of the New York State Normal School," and
the topics "Defects in the Training of Teachers" and "The Kindergarten"
were discussed.

On Thursday the topics "History and Geography in Training Class"
and "Professional Progress" were discussed, and Superintendent D. L.
Bardwell, of New York City, made a address on "Training for Efficiency."

On Friday "Observation and Practice Work of the Training Schools"
was treated from three points of view: (a) Normal; (b) Training School;
(c) Training Class; and Dr. T. B. Stowell, of the Potsdam Normal School,
read a paper on "The Function of the Training Department of the Normal
School."

After election of officers for the year 1906 the section adjourned.

The Development of the New York State Normal School

BY FRANCIS J. CHENEY, CORTLAND NORMAL SCHOOL.

To most of you it is, without doubt, a familiar story I have to tell,
and yet may not be without profit to repeat it.

The normal school as an institution has existed less than 300 years.
The first organization for the training of teachers, of which we have any
account, believed to have been established by La Salle at Reims, in about
the year 1683. A few years after the efforts of La Salle to furnish an
opportunity to those who wished to prepare themselves for teaching,
Augustus Hermann Ranke established a teachers' class at Halle, in
Prussia. He provided free board for such poor students as stood in need
of assistance, and selected out of the whole number twelve who exhibited
the right basis of piety, knowledge, skill and desire for teaching, and
constituted them his "Seminarium Preceptorium," or teachers' Seminary.
These pupil teachers received instruction for two years in methods, and,
in return, promised to teach for three years in the institution after
nishing their course. So successful was this work, that it is said, hundreds
of teachers were attracted from all parts of Europe to observe and study
the organization, methods and spirit of the school.

Other classes for teachers were opened in other parts of Germany.
Courses of lectures on the best methods of teaching German and the
ancient languages became common in the universities and higher schools
of Germany. From Germany the system spread to other parts of Europe;
but the normal school idea, we understand it, made little headway until
the beginning of the nineteenth century.

It is a mooted question how far these efforts in Europe to train
teachers influenced the normal school idea in the United States. Some
contend that they had no influence upon us whatever, as the earliest
advocates of the normal school in this country knew little or nothing of
what had been done in Europe. Although the seed was planted here as
early as 1816, it required time to take root. In that year a young man,
